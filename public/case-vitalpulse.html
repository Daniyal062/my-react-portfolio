01 — The Problem

Data Was Creating
Anxiety, Not Motivation

Fitness apps were designed by people who loved metrics. The result: dashboards that looked like cockpits — intimidating before you'd even taken your first step. A referenced study showed 71% of fitness app users felt "inadequate" when opening their health app, regardless of whether their actual metrics were good or bad.

The brief was precise: redesign the core feedback loop. Make health data feel encouraging, not clinical. Make every open feel like a small win — even on bad days.

71%

Felt Inadequate

Users who felt "not good enough" when opening a fitness app — regardless of actual health performance.

14d

Average Churn

Most fitness app users who receive no encouragement within the first 2 weeks stop using the app entirely.

6→1

Homepage Metrics

The redesign reduced visible homepage metrics from 6 competing data points to 1 dominant daily score.

02 — Research Methodology

A Diary Study Revealed
the Emotional Pattern

Standard usability testing would miss the core issue — this was a problem of emotional experience over time, not task completion. I ran a 2-week diary study with 18 participants. They logged how they felt before and after opening their health app, using a simple emoji scale. The pattern was consistent and stark.

📋
Week 1 · Days 1–3
Initial Excitement

Participants started with high motivation. They opened the app 4–6× daily. Emotional response: 😊 → 😐. The novelty carried them past the complexity.

📉
Week 1 · Days 4–7
The Friction Cliff

App opens dropped to 1–2×/day. Participants described feeling like they were "doing homework" rather than tracking health. The visual complexity was demanding cognitive effort they didn't want to spend.

😰
Week 2 · Days 8–11
Anxiety Peak

13 of 18 participants reported feeling anxious when opening the app. The emotional response wasn't triggered by bad numbers — it was triggered by the visual density alone, before they'd even read a single metric.

🚪
Week 2 · Days 12–14
Abandonment

11 of 18 participants had effectively stopped using the app by day 14. Not because they stopped caring about their health — but because the app made them feel worse, not better.

Core Insight

"Users experience interfaces emotionally first, rationally second. If the emotional response on open is negative — complexity, overwhelm, inadequacy — no amount of useful data will retain them."

03 — Wireframes

The Score-First
Approach

The central wireframe decision: replace the metric grid with a single circular score. Everything else becomes secondary, revealed progressively. The ring metaphor was chosen because it's universally understood (progress, completion, achievement) without requiring any explanation.

04 — Visual Design

Soft, Warm, Alive.

The visual direction is intentionally therapeutic. Warm whites, soft amethyst purples, and generous rounded forms that feel organic rather than clinical. The entire palette was tested in user interviews for "approachability" — if a color felt cold or medical, it was eliminated.

Design Decision — Bad Day Mode

The fourth screen shows the most important design decision: how to handle a below-average score. Rather than showing a disappointing number without context, the app reframes it as part of a journey. "Not your best — that's OK" tested 3× more positively than simply showing "62" in red. Language is design.

05 — Results

Retention Told
the Story

We ran the redesign as an A/B test against the original for 4 weeks. The primary metric was day-14 retention — were users still actively using the app two weeks after first open? This is the industry-standard churn benchmark for health apps.

4×

Week-3 Retention

Users who personalized their own goals showed 4× higher week-3 retention vs. those on default settings.

+64%

Daily Opens

Average daily app opens increased 64% vs. the control. Users were checking in more, not less, with less data shown.

9.1/10

Satisfaction Score

Post-redesign satisfaction score vs. 4.2/10 baseline. The most common word in feedback: "encouraging."

06 — What I Learned

How Emotional Design
Changed My Practice

01
Frame the story, not just the data

The number 87 means nothing. "Excellent today" means everything. Users experience your interface emotionally first, rationally second. Your copy and framing matter as much as your layout — possibly more. Language is the invisible design layer.

02
Metaphors are your fastest onboarding

Not a single user in testing asked what the ring meant. They immediately understood progress and completion. When your visual metaphor is right, you eliminate instruction — and instruction is always friction. Design for instant understanding over described understanding.

03
Personalization is a retention strategy

Users who set their own goals stayed 4× longer. Giving someone ownership of their experience makes them accountable to it. Customization isn't a power-user feature — it's a psychological commitment device that the majority of users need to stay engaged long-term.
