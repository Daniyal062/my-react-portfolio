01 — The Problem

Users Were Abandoning
Mid-Session

Legacy banking apps were designed in an era when more data meant more value. The result: screens crammed with figures, charts, and CTAs all competing for attention simultaneously — leaving users confused, anxious, and ultimately inactive.

Heatmap analysis revealed that 68% of users opened the app but completed no intended action. The drop-off wasn't a trust issue — it was a clarity issue. Users didn't know where to look first.

02 — Research

Two Users, Two
Completely Different Needs

I ran 24 user interviews across two distinct user groups. Their mental models of money were fundamentally different — and the original app was serving neither group well. The insight that changed everything: one app was trying to be two apps simultaneously.

Primary User · 78% of base
DAILY
TRANSACTOR
Checks app 5–8× daily

Need: Speed above everything. They want to see balance, send money, done. Every extra tap is friction. They abandon if a flow takes more than 3 steps. Dense data feels like a punishment.

"I just want to know my balance and send my sister money. Why does it take 6 screens?"
Secondary User · 22% of base
PASSIVE
TRACKER
Checks app 2–4× monthly

Need: Context and clarity. They want to understand spending trends, not just balances. Monthly reviews require digestible charts and export options. For this group, data density is welcome — when organized.

"I want to see where my money went this month. One screen, organized by category."
Key Insight

"The original app tried to serve both groups on the same screen. The redesign uses a mode-based home: Quick View for transactors, Deep View for trackers. Same data — different density."

05 — Results

Numbers That Moved

After implementing the redesign across a staged rollout, we tracked key behavioral metrics over a 4-week period. The results validated the core hypothesis: reducing complexity dramatically increased completion rates.

06 — What I Learned

Three Lessons That
Changed How I Design

01
Hierarchy is the product

The single most impactful change wasn't the visual style — it was the decision of what NOT to show on the home screen. Removing 6 elements and promoting 1 reduced cognitive load more than any visual treatment could have. Subtraction is design.

02
Color is a contract

In finance, users build unconscious contracts with color. Red means loss. Green means gain. Using these colors decoratively — even subtly — caused measurable anxiety in testing. Every color decision must earn its meaning before it earns its place.

03
Speed is a feature

The fastest interaction is no interaction. I became obsessed with the "0-tap experience" — what can the user know before they tap anything? Designing for scanning first reduced task completion time by 34% and felt like a magic trick to users.
